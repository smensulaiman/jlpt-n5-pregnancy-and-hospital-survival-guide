Chapter Fifteen

Pharmacy

薬局 — Yakkyoku

15.1 Situation 場面

In Japan the hospital does not usually give you the medicine. It gives you a prescription (処方箋, shohōsen), which you take to a pharmacy (調剤薬局, chōzai yakkyoku) — normally right next door. You will also need a drugstore (ドラッグストア) for diapers, baby soap, formula, and a thermometer.

15.8 Cultural Notes 文化ノート

Japanese Pharmacies

  • Get an お薬手帳. This free booklet records every medicine you have ever been prescribed. Pharmacists check it for dangerous interactions, and showing it can lower your fee. Get one for your wife and one for the baby.
  • The prescription expires. A Japanese 処方箋 is valid for only 4 days including the day it was issued. Do not put it in a drawer.
  • Pharmacists really do explain. They will tell you dose, timing, and side effects, and hand you a printed sheet. If you don't understand, ask: 「ゆっくりお願いします。」
  • Drugstores are cheaper than hospital shops. Chains like Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Sundrug, and Welcia sell diapers and formula far cheaper. Bulk diapers are cheapest online or at Akachan Honpo / Nishimatsuya (baby superstores).
  • 液体ミルク (liquid formula) is a relatively new and genuinely useful product in Japan — no hot water, no mixing. Perfect for night feeds and emergencies. Keep some at home.
  • お大事に (odaiji ni) is what staff say as you leave. You don't need to reply with more than 「ありがとうございます。」
