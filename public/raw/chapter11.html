Chapter Eleven

Hospital Stay

入院生活 — Nyūin seikatsu

11.1 Situation 場面

In Japan a normal delivery means a hospital stay of about five days (a C-section, about a week). That is five days of meals, showers, laundry, Wi-Fi questions, air conditioning, room cleaning, and visitors. This chapter gives you the everyday sentences of hospital life.

11.8 Cultural Notes 文化ノート

Living in a Japanese Hospital

  • Silence is the rule. Japanese wards are extremely quiet. Consider 9 p.m. (消灯, lights out) an absolute boundary. No phone calls in rooms, no loud voices in corridors, ever.
  • Bring 100-yen coins. Laundry, dryers, some TVs, and lockers all run on coins. Keep a small bag of ¥100 coins in the hospital bag — this is a genuinely useful piece of advice.
  • Hospital food is part of the care. Maternity hospitals in Japan are famous for excellent food — some serve a celebratory "お祝い膳" (oiwai-zen, celebration meal) before discharge. Family meals are usually not included, so eat at the shop or bring food.
  • Visiting is a formality. Visitors sign in at reception, wear a visitor badge, sanitize hands, and wear a mask. Small children are frequently banned outright. Warn relatives in advance so nobody is turned away at the door.
  • Gifts: Visitors often bring a small gift (お見舞い, omimai). Flowers are sometimes prohibited in maternity wards (pollen/infection). Sweets or fruit are safer.
  • The husband's role. Bring clean clothes, take dirty laundry home, refill drinks, and — most valuable of all — let her sleep.
