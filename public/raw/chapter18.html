Chapter Eighteen

Emergency Situations

緊急のとき — Kinkyū no toki

18.1 Situation 場面

This is the chapter you hope never to use. Learn it anyway. It covers a newborn's fever, heavy bleeding in the mother, a baby refusing milk, breathing difficulty, and how to call an ambulance or a night clinic. Read section 18.3 out loud until you can say it without looking.

Emergency numbers — put these in your phone tonight

  • 119 — Fire & ambulance (救急車). Free. Say: 救急です。 (Kyūkyū desu.)
  • #8000 — Nationwide pediatric after-hours phone advice (子ども医療電話相談). Nurses/doctors advise you whether to go to hospital tonight. Japanese only in most areas, but a lifeline.
  • #7119 — Emergency consultation line (available in many areas): "Should I call an ambulance?"
  • Your hospital's night number — write it here and stick it on the fridge.

18.7 Cultural Notes 文化ノート

Emergencies in Japan — What You Must Know

  • A fever in a baby under 3 months is always an emergency. 38°C or above in a newborn = go to hospital, at any hour. Do not "wait until morning."
  • Ambulances are free but not taxis. Japanese ambulances cost nothing, but calling one for a minor problem is frowned upon and slows the system. Use #7119 or #8000 if you're unsure.
  • Ambulances ask which hospital. The crew will ask 「かかりつけの病院はありますか」 ("Do you have a regular hospital?"). Know the name of your clinic and say it clearly. Have it written down in Japanese in your wallet.
  • Danger signs in a newborn — go immediately: fever 38°C+, limp/unresponsive (ぐったり), blue lips (唇が青い), fast or labored breathing, refusing all feeds, convulsions (けいれん), a bulging soft spot, no wet diaper for 12 hours.
  • Danger signs in the mother: soaking a pad in under an hour, large clots, fever with chills, severe headache with blurred vision (可能性: postpartum preeclampsia), a hot painful red breast, calf pain and swelling, or thoughts of harming herself or the baby.
  • Prepare a "go card." Write in Japanese: your name, address, phone, your wife's name, the hospital name, allergies, and blood types. Keep it in your wallet. In a panic, hand over the card.
