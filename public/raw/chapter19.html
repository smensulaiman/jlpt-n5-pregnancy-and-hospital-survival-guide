19.1 Situation 場面

Life with a newborn means the convenience store at 3 a.m., taxis with a baby, the drugstore, and — most importantly — the city office (市役所), where you must register the birth within 14 days and claim your benefits. This chapter covers all of it, plus nursery school and parking.

19.7 Cultural Notes 文化ノート

Daily Life With a Baby in Japan

  • The 14-day birth registration is a hard deadline. Go to the 市役所 with: 出生証明書 (from the hospital), 母子手帳, your 在留カード, your 印鑑, and your bankbook. Do all four procedures in one visit — birth registration, child allowance, infant medical certificate, health insurance.
  • 乳幼児医療証 — free healthcare for the baby. Most municipalities make medical care free (or nearly) for infants. Carry this card with the insurance card whenever you go to a clinic.
  • Foreign names go in katakana. On the birth registration, a foreign child's name is written in katakana (and romaji). Decide the spelling in advance — it becomes official.
  • Also register the birth with your own embassy and apply for the child's passport and (if applicable) a Japanese residence status. The hospital will not remind you.
  • 保育園 (nursery) is competitive. In cities, applications open in the autumn for the following April, and demand far exceeds supply (待機児童 problem). Apply early — even if the baby is only a few months old.
  • Baby rooms are everywhere. Department stores, malls, train stations, and even highway rest stops have ベビールーム with changing tables, hot water for formula, and private nursing booths. They are free and clean. Japan is genuinely excellent at this.
